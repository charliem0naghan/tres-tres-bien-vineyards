The Barrels

First-use French oak. Twelve to eighteen months of quiet transformation. Where wine becomes what it is meant to be.

The Oak

First-use French oak,
chosen deliberately

The choice of barrel is not an afterthought at Tres Très Bien — it is a winemaking decision as fundamental as the choice of variety, the timing of harvest, or the length of fermentation. We use first-use French oak exclusively, and the decision is rooted in a clear conviction: we want the barrel to serve the wine, not dominate it.

A first-use barrel — one that has never held wine before — is the most active of all oak vessels. Its wood is still dense with the compounds that will migrate slowly into the wine over the months of aging: tannins that soften the wine's own harsh phenolics, vanillin that adds a subtle creamy note to the mid-palate, toasty spice that complements the dark fruit character of Cabernet Franc and Malbec. The effect is integration — a rounding of what the young wine's hard edges, a deepening of what was already there.

French oak carries a finer grain than American oak, and the compounds it releases are gentler, more subtle, more complex. It does not shout. It whispers — adding a dimension of flavour that the perceptive taster can sense, but that never obscures the character of the fruit itself.

"The barrel is not decoration. It is an ingredient — measured, considered, and chosen with care."

Slow time in

The cellar at Los Chacayes maintains a steady low temperature year-round — a natural consequence of the altitude that slows every biochemical process and allows each vintage to evolve at its own measured pace. The wine is never rushed. It is tasted, assessed, and left in peace to become itself.

The Aging

Twelve months of
quiet patience

After fermentation, the new wine is transferred into barrel. It will remain there — resting, evolving, slowly integrating — for between twelve and eighteen months, depending on the vintage and the wine. Cabernet Franc tends toward the longer end; it benefits from the extended contact with oak, which softens its tannins and allows its complex aromatics to fully emerge. Malbec is assessed vintage by vintage: some years the fruit is so expressive and complete that it needs less time; others, the structure demands a longer conversation with the wood.

During this period, the barrels are topped regularly to replace any wine lost to evaporation — the so-called "angel's share." The wine is tasted frequently. It is never manipulated. We do not fine, filter, or adjust unless a problem demands it. The barrel does the work that heavy-handed winemaking would otherwise attempt with additives and machinery.

When the wine is finally ready — and this is a decision based entirely on taste, not on schedule — it is racked, blended across barrels to ensure consistency, and prepared for bottling. The barrels are then retired. They will not be used again.

Oak Comparison

Why first-use French oak

Our Choice

First-Use French Oak

Nevers & Allier Forest, France
Tannin Contribution Maximum — the new wood releases its full complement of tannins, softening the wine's structure over 12–18 months
Flavour Complexity Subtle vanilla, toasty spice, and a creamy mid-palate character that complements the fruit without overpowering it
Oxygen Exchange Slow and controlled — French oak's fine grain allows a gentle micro-oxygenation that stabilises colour and integrates tannins
Single Use Policy Each barrel is used for one vintage only, then retired — ensuring maximum contribution to every wine we make
Not Our Approach

Neutral Oak (2nd–3rd Fill)

Reused barrels, diminished influence
Tannin Contribution Minimal to none — the wood's soluble compounds have been largely exhausted by previous vintages
Flavour Complexity Little oak flavour imparted — used deliberately in some winery styles where pure fruit expression is the goal
Oxygen Exchange Oxygen exchange still occurs through the wood's pores, but the flavour integration benefits of new oak are absent
Cost Significantly lower cost per barrel — a practical choice for larger production volumes, but not aligned with our philosophy
