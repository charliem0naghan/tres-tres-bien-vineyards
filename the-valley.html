The Geography

A valley against
the Andes

The Uco Valley sits approximately 100 kilometres south of Mendoza city, compressed between the eastern slopes of the Andes and the wide, semi-arid plain of the Argentine interior. It is not a single place so much as a collection of sub-zones, each with its own altitude, soil type, and microclimate — joined by the common thread of high elevation and the Andean proximity that defines the entire valley.

The geography here is extreme. Altitude ranges from around 900 to over 1,500 metres above sea level. The air is dry and thin; ultraviolet radiation is intense. Rainfall averages barely 200 millimetres annually, making every vineyard dependent on the irrigation systems fed by Andean snowmelt. The rivers and canals that carry this water — the Río Tunuyán and its tributaries — are the lifeblood of the valley.

The climate that results from this geography is precise and powerful. Long, warm, intensely sunny days build sugar and flavour. Cold nights — temperatures that can drop eighteen degrees Celsius or more between noon and midnight in the weeks before harvest — lock in acidity and preserve the volatile aromatic compounds that give these wines their character. Nothing about this climate is casual. It is extreme, and the wines reflect that.

"The Uco Valley is not a gentle place. Its wines are not gentle wines. They carry the weight and beauty of the landscape that made them."

The gorges

The name Los Chacayes refers to the quebradas — gorges — that cut down from the Andes into the upper Uco Valley. This is among the highest and most dramatically situated vineyard land in Mendoza, and it produces wines of a precision and mineral quality that set it apart from all other sub-zones of the region.

Los Chacayes

The finest corner
of the valley

Within the Uco Valley, no sub-zone has attracted more attention in recent years than Los Chacayes. Situated in the upper reaches of the Tunuyán department, at elevations that push above 1,100 metres in our own parcels and beyond 1,300 metres in the highest sites nearby, Los Chacayes sits at the intersection of altitude, volcanic soil, and the direct influence of the Andean snowfields.

The soils here are primarily alluvial — ancient, coarse, and well-drained, laid down by glacial processes that predate the modern Andes by millions of years. Below the surface gravel and sandy loam lies a deeper structure of clay and volcanic rock that retains moisture through the dry summer months and forces vines to develop deep root systems in search of water. This stress is productive. Vines under controlled stress build concentration in their fruit and complexity in their flavour.

In the twenty-first century, Los Chacayes has become associated with a particular style of Malbec and Cabernet Franc — wines of unusual aromatic clarity, fine tannin structure, and a mineral precision that distinguishes them from wines grown at lower elevations elsewhere in Mendoza. It is a style that the altitude imposes, not one that the winemaker invents. We are grateful for that imposition.
