The Varietals

French grapes,
Argentine soul

Cabernet Franc and Malbec are, at their core, French. Cabernet Franc thrives in the Loire Valley; Malbec built its original reputation in Cahors. Yet both have found in Mendoza's Uco Valley something that France cannot always offer — intensity of sunshine, purity of air, and an extreme altitude that stretches the ripening season into something unhurried and complete.

At Tres Très Bien, we chose these two varieties deliberately. Cabernet Franc gives us structure, aromatics — that distinctive note of graphite and red currant — and a long finish shaped by the cold Andean nights. Malbec offers depth of colour, plush texture, and a quality of fruit that no other place on earth quite replicates at this elevation.

Together and separately, they tell the story of Los Chacayes: a landscape of gravel-loam soils, blinding sun, and nights cold enough to halt every biological clock and concentrate every flavour the vine has worked all day to build.

"Altitude is the winemaker here. We simply follow its instructions."

The Families, Tres Très Bien Vineyards

Cabernet Franc

Loire Valley origins · Los Chacayes, Mendoza

The flagship of our estate. At 1,100 metres, Cabernet Franc develops an aromatic complexity — violets, graphite, dried herbs — that is impossible to achieve on the valley floor. Structure comes naturally here; the diurnal range preserves every note of acidity and finesse.

Malbec

Cahors origins · Los Chacayes, Mendoza

Our high-altitude Malbec is a different animal from the Malbec grown at lower elevations in Mendoza. Slower ripening builds layers of flavour. The tannins are fine and polished. The colour is deep. The finish carries a mineral quality — the taste of volcanic rock, gravel, and mountain air.

Los Chacayes · Tunuyán · Uco Valley
Soil, stone, and the
shape of the Andes
The soils of Los Chacayes are alluvial — washed down over millennia from the mountains above. Beneath the thin topsoil lies gravel, stone, and a subsoil that drains to near-perfection. Vines must work for every drop of moisture, sending roots deep and building concentration in every berry.
Est. 2016 · 100% Estate Fruit

The Terroir

Old vines,
new plantings

When our three founding families acquired the Los Chacayes parcels in 2016, they inherited land with a history. Some of the older vines on the property had been cultivated by previous growers who understood what this corner of Tunuyán could offer. The estate's earliest plantings carry in their wood a living memory of the years before us — of Argentine winters and summers, of hand-hoeing and dry-farming techniques passed down through the local community.

The new plantings we have made since 2016 complement these older vines with careful attention to clonal selection and vine density. Planting more densely forces competition between vines, reducing yields and concentrating flavour. Every decision in the vineyard is made with the same question: what does the wine need?

The combination of old and new — established root systems drawing from deep in the subsoil, and younger vines carrying the specific clonal character we have selected — creates the complexity in the finished wine that we most prize. Neither alone would give us what we seek. Together, they represent Tres Très Bien's particular expression of this landscape.
