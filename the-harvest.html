The Harvest

Every grape, hand-picked. Every decision, considered. The story of how Tres Très Bien brings the fruit of Los Chacayes from vine to vessel.

The Approach

Every grape,
by hand

There is no machine harvest at Tres Très Bien. It has never been considered. The decision to pick every grape by hand is not merely a romantic preference for tradition — it is a practical commitment to quality that shapes every aspect of how we farm and how we vinify.

In the Mendoza calendar, harvest falls in March and April — the southern hemisphere's late summer turning toward autumn. In Los Chacayes, the altitude delays this timing slightly. Our grapes ripen slowly and deliberately, accumulating sugars, phenolics, and aromatics across a longer growing season than is possible at lower elevations. When we finally begin to pick, the decision of timing is everything.

Ripeness is assessed daily in the final weeks before harvest. We taste the grapes. We measure sugar and acidity. We look at the seeds and skins. We wait. And then — at the precise moment when flavour, structure, and freshness are in perfect balance — we pick.

"The vine tells you when it is ready. The harvest crew must be listening."

Harvest philosophy, Tres Très Bien Vineyards

At Los Chacayes, we harvest through the night. Headlamps move through the rows in the small hours, hands working with practiced efficiency in the dark. By dawn, the bins are loaded and moving to the cellar — chilled by the cold Andean night air, the fruit entirely intact.
Night temperatures at harvest: approximately 8°C

Night Harvesting

Picking before
the sun rises

In the Uco Valley, daytime temperatures during harvest can climb above 30°C. Heat is the enemy of aromatics — the volatile compounds that give wine its fragrance and complexity begin to dissipate the moment a grape reaches room temperature. At Tres Très Bien, we harvest at night to preserve every molecule of aroma the vine has built.

Night harvesting is not comfortable. It demands a committed team who understand what they are doing and why. Our picking crew works in the hours between two and six in the morning, when the air temperature at altitude hovers near eight degrees Celsius. At this temperature, the fruit arrives at the cellar essentially pre-chilled — no refrigerated storage required before processing begins.

The difference in the final wine is tangible. Aromatics are brighter and more precise. Freshness is retained even at full ripeness. The perfume that rises from a glass of our Cabernet Franc — that violet and graphite note, that dried herb fragrance — is a direct consequence of picking it when the night air was cool and the stars were still visible above the Andes.

From Vine to Vat

The harvest process

Five careful steps, each one protecting the integrity of what the vineyard has grown.

01
February – March

Monitor Ripeness

Daily tasting and weekly laboratory analysis of sugar, pH, and total acidity. The picking date is never predetermined — it emerges from an ongoing conversation with the fruit. We taste, measure, and wait until the window of perfect balance opens.

02
2am – 6am

Night Pick

The harvest crew enters the vineyard under headlamps. Every cluster is cut by hand using harvest shears and placed carefully into small picking bins. No mechanical handling. No rough treatment. Each berry arrives at the sorting table essentially unbruised.

03
Cellar · Dawn

Sort by Hand

As the bins arrive at the cellar, fruit is spread across the sorting table and inspected cluster by cluster. Damaged, under-ripe, or overripe grapes are removed. Only the finest clusters proceed. This step is painstaking — and entirely worth it.

04
24–72 Hours

Cold Soak

Before fermentation begins, the crushed fruit rests under refrigeration. This cold soak extracts colour and aromatic compounds from the skins without extracting bitter tannins — a technique that gives our wines their deep colour and fragrant nose while maintaining elegance and finesse.

05
7–14 Days

Begin Fermentation

Native and selected yeasts are introduced to begin fermentation. The vat is punched down twice daily to integrate skins and juice. Temperature is carefully controlled. What emerges, after twelve to fourteen days, is the young wine — raw, vibrant, full of potential, and ready for the barrel cellar.
